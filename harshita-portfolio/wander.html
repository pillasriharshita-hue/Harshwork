01 — Overview

What Wander is. Who it's for. What it had to do.

Wander is a location-based walking app that helps locals explore nearby places through curiosity, light social connection, and optional rewards — not step targets, streak pressure, or performance metrics.

The product targets the gap between "I should walk more" and "I actually did." That gap isn't motivational — it's structural. If the walk itself becomes rewarding through discovery and meaning, movement follows without needing to be the point.

Ownership Statement

I led user research, research synthesis, experience strategy, interaction design, and both rounds of usability testing. I collaborated with a 4-person team on wireframe iteration, visual design, prototyping, and final screens.

Product terminology:

Outpost
Location-based discovery point. Physical spots that become interactive when approached.
Echo
A friend's message or memory anchored to a specific place. Passive social, not performative.
Quest
Optional walk-based mission. Never mandatory. Designed to reduce not increase pressure.
Resource
Collectible item earned through exploration. Feeds avatar and profile progression.
Companion
Guided wayfinding character. Appears during navigation to reduce friction, not add pressure.
02 — The Problem

Every existing solution was solving the wrong problem.

Fitness walking apps are built on a shared premise: people don't walk enough because they lack motivation. The solution they offer is structured accountability: streaks, goals, performance tracking, badges.

That premise doesn't hold. The behavioral science literature consistently points elsewhere: people's relationship with walking collapses not because motivation disappears but because the enabling environment does.

People who stopped walking regularly hadn't lost the will to walk. They'd lost the reason the walk felt worth doing.

The Design Gap

The opportunity wasn't another motivation layer.

It was redesigning what the walk itself offers — so that the experience of walking is the point, not a means to a step count.

Can curiosity, discovery, and ambient social presence make a neighborhood walk feel worth taking on its own terms?

03 — Research

What the literature said. What the interviews showed.

7 academic papers on motivation, social retention, and AR engagement. 7 user interviews with lapsed, inconsistent, and active walkers. Research happened before any wireframe.

Who we talked to

The Lapsed Structured Walker
Had a habit. Lost the scaffold.

Had gym or class routine. Life transition removed the structure. Motivation unchanged. Access and habit anchor gone.

The Social-Dependent Mover
Only walks with someone.

Movement happens when bundled with a social activity. Remove the partner, the habit vanishes.

The Passive Decompressor
Uses screen for recovery.

After cognitively demanding days, screens are the default recovery. Needs a behavioral bridge between couch and movement.

The Seasonal Mover
Habit breaks with conditions.

Activity contingent on weather, walking partner, accessible outdoor space. Habitual until the enabler disappeared.

The Goal-Fatigue Dropout
Burned out on tracking.

Used fitness apps. Streak pressure, missed-goal guilt, and notification overload killed the habit faster than no app at all.

The Curious Explorer
Walks when there's a reason.

Consistently active when walking serves a purpose. Remove the purpose, remove the walk. Discovery is the actual motivator.

Recurring themes

Motivation wasn't missing

Every participant wanted to walk more. The deficit was structural — enabling conditions disappeared, so behavior collapsed.

"I used to do CrossFit every week. When my schedule changed I just... never went back."

Walks feel purposeless

Walking for its own sake felt hollow to most participants. Walks that "went somewhere" were far more sustainable.

"I'll walk to get somewhere. Or if someone's with me. But just... walking? For nothing?"

Social = strongest sustainer

Every participant who maintained walking habits had a social component. Remove it, the habit vanishes.

"Knowing someone's waiting for me is the only reason I show up some days."

Pressure is counterproductive

Streak pressure and missed-goal guilt accelerated dropout. Apps were abandoned not because they weren't sophisticated enough.

"The day I missed a streak was the day I deleted the app."

04 — Design Principles

THREE PRINCIPLES.
EACH ONE A TRADEOFF.

Each principle came from research. Each one required giving something up. Seniority is visible in tradeoffs, not just in what you built.

01
Discovery over performance

The walk should offer something — a reveal, a place, a story — not just log a metric. Intrinsic reward, not extrinsic tracking.

What research showed
Walks without purpose felt hollow. Discovery created the reason to keep going.
What we designed
Mystery Outposts — partial information revealed only on approach.
What we avoided
Full POI visibility on the map. Tested it — showing all locations removed the reason to go.
02
Social presence without social pressure

Other people make habits stick — but competitive or performative social accelerates dropout. The goal was ambient connection.

What research showed
Every participant who maintained a habit had a social component. But competitive apps made dropout worse.
What we designed
Echoes — friend messages anchored to locations. Visible only at the place. Passive, not competitive.
What we avoided
Step leaderboards, shared activity logs, public walking history.
03
Optionality over obligation

Every gamification element is optional. Opt-in or completely ignorable. No failure state. No guilt messaging. No streak penalty.

What research showed
Missing a streak goal caused immediate dropout. Pressure made people feel worse than not tracking at all.
What we designed
Quests always optional. Resources accumulate passively. No notification for missed progress.
What we avoided
Mandatory goals, daily targets, missed-day notifications.
05 — Design Process

Three decisions that defined the product.

The process is reconstructed around its decisions — not every sketch, not every iteration. Each decision resolved a question that research raised but didn't answer.

What should the map show before you start walking?
Option A — RejectedFull Outpost visibility. Every nearby location shown upfront.
Option B — ChosenMystery Outposts. Partial preview only. Full reveal requires physical approach.
In prototype testing, showing all locations removed the reason to go to any specific one. Mystery created the pull. Users who saw partial previews changed their walking routes.
How should social feel — shared, competitive, or ambient?
Option A — RejectedGlobal activity feed. See what friends walked, where they went, how many steps.
Option B — ChosenLocation-anchored Echoes. Friend messages visible only at the specific place.
Social accountability sustains habits — but competitive social accelerates dropout. Anchoring to location made social presence feel personal rather than performative.
Should gamification be central or optional?
Option A — RejectedGamification-forward. Quests required, streaks tracked, goals set at onboarding.
Option B — ChosenFully optional. Resources accumulate passively. Quests surface as suggestions.
Every participant who'd used and abandoned walking apps cited pressure and missed-goal guilt as the dropout trigger. Optionality allowed the product to work for both the casual explorer and the quest-completionist.

06 — Delivered

The four systems that make the experience coherent.

Each system solves a specific problem identified in research — and each one is annotated with which research finding it answers.

FEATURE 01
Mystery Outpost Map

Home screen anchored to a map showing nearby Outposts with partial information only. Full details revealed on physical approach.

Answers: "walks feel purposeless." Mystery creates the reason to go somewhere specific.
FEATURE 02
AR Discovery + Companion

On approach, AR mode reveals the Outpost in full. Companion guides without directing. Content unlocks passively.

Answers: "walking needs to offer something." AR turns the physical location into the reward.
FEATURE 03
Echoes — Ambient Social

Friend messages anchored to specific locations. Visible only at the place. No global feed. No performance comparison.

Answers: "social sustains habits without competitive pressure."
FEATURE 04
Optional Quests + Resource Loop

Walk-based missions surface as suggestions, never requirements. Resources accumulate passively. No goals or deadlines.

Answers: "pressure causes dropout faster than no app."

08 — What Wander Proved

Thesis Evaluation

The thesis: curiosity and ambient social can make a neighborhood walk feel worth taking without framing walking as performance. Here's an honest evaluation.

What held
  • Mystery mechanic created genuine pull — users changed walking routes for Outposts they couldn't fully see
  • Ambient social (Echoes) felt more personal than competitive
  • Optionality reduced pressure without reducing engagement
  • Tutorial restructuring resolved comprehension issues without adding friction
What didn't fully hold
  • AR fidelity mismatch — prototype couldn't replicate real-world AR behavior
  • Reward loop visibility — Resource collection didn't close itself for casual users
  • Terminology inconsistency persisted in mid-fidelity
  • Onboarding timing improved but still front-loaded
What I'd test next
  • Real-world AR validation — lab testing can't substitute for street-level AR use
  • Longitudinal behavior testing — does the habit form over 2 to 4 weeks?
  • Narrower MVP scope — Outpost discovery alone as a testable first release
  • Naming system locked before next prototype round

Open product questions

01

Does the curiosity mechanic sustain after the city has been explored? Is Wander a product for locals who haven't explored their own city — or one that requires new content to remain engaging?

02

At what point does ambient social become social obligation? The line between "knowing someone was here" and "feeling like you should be here too" may narrow with a larger friend network.

03

Does making walking non-performative actually make it more habitual — or does it remove the accountability that keeps people honest?

The most important thing this project taught me

The decision that was hardest to defend — hiding location information from users who said they wanted more of it — turned out to be the decision that made the product work. Users said they wanted to see all locations. Their behavior said otherwise. I trusted the behavior.
