UttishaNow

Designed a behavior-first mobile experience to help young adults sit less.

UttishaNow is a mobile UX case study exploring how smart reminders, habit cues, and low-pressure progress feedback can help sedentary young adults reduce long sitting periods without disrupting school, work, or daily life. Instead of treating movement as a motivation problem, this project reframes it as a routine-design problem: how might we make movement easier to start when the day is already busy?

PROBLEM

The day collapses at 1 PM.

For many young adults, sedentary behavior does not happen because they intentionally avoid movement. It happens because sitting becomes the default.

Classes, remote work, screens, assignments, meetings, and deadlines create long blocks of stillness. By the time users realize they have been sitting too long, movement already feels like another task to manage.

The challenge was not simply: How do we remind people to move?

The deeper challenge was: How do we design movement into a routine without making users feel interrupted, guilty, or overwhelmed?

SOLUTION

Design movement into the routine, not on top of it.

UttishaNow helps users take small movement breaks through smart reminders, habit cues, and simple progress feedback that fit naturally into their day. The experience focuses on three things:

Timely nudges

Reminders appear around moments when movement is more realistic, not randomly during deep focus.

Small actions

Users are given quick, manageable movement options instead of being pushed into a workout mindset.

Low-pressure progress

Progress feedback encourages consistency without guilt, streak anxiety, or perfection.

Why this matters: Sedentary behavior is often framed as a personal discipline issue. But through this project, I found that the bigger issue is environmental. Young adults are not always choosing to sit for long hours — their routines are designed around sitting. If the environment keeps pulling users back into sitting, the product should create small openings for movement inside that same environment.

01 — RESEARCH

What I wanted to understand.

Before designing, I wanted to understand why young adults remain sedentary even when they already know movement is good for them. I focused on four research questions:

  • When do young adults realize they have been sitting too long?
  • What stops them from taking movement breaks?
  • What kinds of reminders feel helpful versus annoying?
  • How can a product support movement without creating pressure?

Research methods.

Secondary research

Reviewed existing research on sedentary behavior, habit formation, and reminder fatigue to understand the behavioral patterns behind long sitting periods.

User conversations

Spoke with young adults who spend long hours sitting for school, work, or personal routines.

Competitive review

Studied wellness, habit, fitness, and reminder-based apps to understand common patterns and gaps.

Journey mapping

Mapped a typical weekday to identify where sitting becomes automatic and where movement could realistically fit.

02 — WHAT I FOUND

Four insights that reshaped the brief.

01

People do not forget movement. They deprioritize it.

Users knew they should move. Movement just rarely felt urgent. When users were focused, tired, or behind schedule, movement became easy to postpone. A common pattern appeared: "I'll move after this task." But one task often became three.

02

Generic reminders are easy to dismiss.

A reminder that says "Time to move" is technically correct, but does not account for context. Users were more open to reminders when they felt timely, small, specific, easy to act on, and non-judgmental.

03

Full workouts felt too large for the moment.

When users were busy, even a 10-minute exercise suggestion felt like too much. They were more likely to respond to micro-actions: stand for 30 seconds, stretch shoulders, refill water, walk around the room.

04

Progress feedback can motivate or shame.

If the product made users feel like they "failed" because they missed a reminder, they were less likely to return. The app should reward returning, not punish missing.

03 — PROBLEM FRAMING

Sedentary behavior is structural, not just behavioral.

The main issue was not that users lacked motivation. The issue was that their daily environments made sitting frictionless and movement effortful. So I reframed the design challenge:

How might we help sedentary young adults build small movement habits that fit into their existing routines instead of asking them to create an entirely new one?

This reframing changed the product direction. Instead of designing a strict fitness tracker, I designed a behavior-support tool that makes movement easier to start.

04 — DESIGN PRINCIPLES

Four principles to guide every screen.

01

Make the action small

The reminder should not ask users to "exercise." It should suggest one easy movement action.

02

Respect the user's context

The product should avoid interrupting users during high-focus moments and should support flexible timing.

03

Encourage without guilt

Missed reminders should not feel like failure. The app should make it easy to restart.

04

Show progress simply

Progress should feel motivating at a glance, not like another dashboard users need to manage.

05 — DESIGN DIRECTION

Design the default. Motivation will follow.

The key shift was moving away from motivation-heavy design. Instead of assuming users needed more willpower, I focused on reducing friction:

  • Make movement prompts easier to act on
  • Make break timing more flexible
  • Make progress feedback encouraging
  • Make the next action obvious
  • Make restarting simple after missed breaks

This changed the product from a reminder app into a routine-support experience.

06 — EXPLORING CONCEPTS

Three directions. One that fit the research.

Rejected

Concept 1 · Strict reminder system

Reminded users at fixed intervals throughout the day. Felt too rigid and ignored real schedules — users might be in class, meetings, or focused work when reminders appeared.

Rejected

Concept 2 · Gamified movement tracker

Used points, streaks, and achievement badges. Risked turning movement into another performance task. For users already feeling tired or busy, this could create pressure instead of support.

Selected ✓

Concept 3 · Flexible movement nudges

Suggested small movement actions based on routine windows and allowed users to snooze, shift, or complete quick movement breaks. Respected the user's day while still helping them act.

07 — KEY USER FLOW

Notice → Choose → Move → Reflect.

The core user flow was designed around a simple loop that made the experience feel manageable and repeatable.

01

Notice

The user receives a gentle movement reminder at a reasonable break point.

02

Choose

The user selects a small movement option based on energy and context.

03

Move

The user completes a short action without needing to start a full workout.

04

Reflect

The app gives simple feedback that reinforces consistency and helps the user return later.

08 — WIREFRAMES & EARLY STRUCTURE

Less dashboard, more action.

In the early wireframes, I focused on reducing decision-making. The first version included a home screen with today's sitting and movement summary, a reminder screen with a movement suggestion, a progress screen showing daily consistency, and a settings flow for reminder preferences.

But the first version had one problem: it still felt too much like a tracking app. Users had to interpret too much information before taking action. So I simplified the hierarchy. The updated version prioritized:

  • What should I do now?
  • How long will it take?
  • Can I delay it?
  • Did I make progress today?

This made the experience more action-oriented and less dashboard-heavy.

09 — DESIGN ITERATION

Three before/after shifts.

Before

The reminder felt generic

The first reminder simply told users it was time to move. Users could dismiss it because it did not feel connected to their situation.

After

Specific and lightweight

The revised reminder offered a small movement action. Example: "You've been sitting for a while. Try a 45-second shoulder reset?" This made the reminder easier to act on because it gave users a clear next step.

Before

Progress focused on completion

The early progress screen emphasized whether users met their daily target. This could make missed breaks feel like failure.

After

Progress focused on returning

The redesigned progress feedback highlighted small wins and recovery. Example: "You moved twice today. One more small break can help reset your afternoon."

Before

Reminder timing felt rigid

The early version used fixed break intervals. Fixed reminders can interrupt classes, meetings, or deep work.

After

Flexible movement windows

The final direction allowed users to set preferred movement windows and adjust reminders based on their daily rhythm. Felt more respectful and realistic.

10 — FINAL EXPERIENCE

Four screens, one loop.

Screen 1

Home screen

The home screen gives users a quick view of their day without overwhelming them. The goal was to help users answer: What can I do right now?

  • Current sitting/movement status
  • Next suggested movement break
  • Simple progress feedback
  • A clear primary action
Screen 2

Smart reminder screen

The reminder screen focuses on one small action at a time. Instead of showing a long list of exercises, it gives a clear suggestion with options to start now, snooze, swap the movement, or mark as done. Supports flexibility without making the user think too much.

Screen 3

Movement options

Movement suggestions are intentionally small and realistic. The design avoids intense workout language because the goal is not performance — it is interruption recovery.

  • Stand and stretch
  • Walk to refill water
  • Do a posture reset
  • Take a two-minute room walk
  • Step outside between tasks
Screen 4

Progress feedback

The progress screen shows consistency in a low-pressure way. Focuses on small wins, movement moments, daily rhythm, and restarting after missed breaks. The product encourages users to continue without making them feel judged.

11 — WHY THESE DECISIONS MATTER

Three deliberate choices behind the design.

01

Avoided guilt-based design

Many habit apps rely on streaks, missed goals, and red warning states. I avoided making failure visually loud because users already struggled with consistency. UttishaNow uses supportive feedback and restart-friendly language.

02

Reduced the action size

A movement habit becomes easier when the first step feels small. That is why the app suggests quick actions rather than full workouts.

03

Designed for real routines

The app does not assume users have perfect schedules. It supports shifting, delaying, and restarting because real days are messy.

12 — VISUAL DESIGN DIRECTION

Calm, focused, slightly energetic.

Dark interface

The dark UI creates a focused experience and reduces visual noise.

Orange accent

Used for action moments, progress, and key highlights. Creates warmth and motion without feeling aggressive.

Clear hierarchy

Large numbers, short prompts, and simple cards reduce scanning effort.

Minimal interaction

The design avoids complex navigation. The goal is to help the user act quickly and return to their day.

13 — EVALUATION

What worked. What improved. What still needs testing.

What worked

The strongest response came from the reminder flow. Users understood the value of receiving small movement suggestions instead of broad fitness prompts. The reminder felt more useful when it gave a specific action.

What improved

The redesigned flow made it easier for users to understand what to do next. The strongest improvement was in perceived ease of starting movement. Users responded better to small, specific nudges than to generic reminders.

Still needs testing

This project still needs deeper validation around long-term use. Next phase should test reminder fatigue over several weeks, whether users continue after missing breaks, personalization based on schedule, accessibility for users with different mobility needs, and integration with calendar or wearable data.

14 — WHAT HELD UP. WHAT DIDN'T. WHAT I'D DO NEXT.

An honest read of the design.

What held up

Movement support works better when it fits into existing routines. Users did not need a product that told them to become more disciplined. They needed a product that made the next movement action easier to start.

→

What did not hold up

The early assumption was that reminders alone could solve the problem. A reminder without context is just another notification. The product needed to support timing, action size, flexibility, and emotional tone.

What I would do next.

01

Test reminder fatigue over time

A one-time usability test can show whether a reminder is understandable, but cannot prove whether users will tolerate it for weeks.

02

Add personalization carefully

Personalization could improve relevance, but too many settings could create friction. I would test lightweight preference setup first.

03

Explore calendar & wearable integration

Calendar data could help avoid bad timing, and wearable data could help detect long sedentary periods more accurately.

15 — REFLECTION

Design the default, not the discipline.

This project taught me that behavior change design is not about pushing users harder. It is about understanding the environment around the behavior.

For UttishaNow, the most important design shift was moving from "How do we motivate users to move?" to "How do we make movement easier to start when life gets busy?"

That shift helped me design a product that supports users without blaming them.

UttishaNow is a reminder that small behavioral shifts often begin with better-designed moments, not bigger goals.
